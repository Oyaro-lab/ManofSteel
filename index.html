The Man Who Builds the House
Is Not the One Burning It Down

Most men are romantics. Most men give everything. Most men would sacrifice their own happiness to keep their families intact. This is not a debate about whether bad men exist. It is a reckoning with what happens to the good ones, and why a society that cannot have this conversation honestly is not protecting anyone.

By Kurt Oyaro  ·  Analysis & Commentary  ·  May 2026

Let us begin with the objection everyone reaches for first, because clearing it out of the way is the only honest way to proceed. Yes, there are men who are abusive. There are narcissists. There are men who abandon their children, who manipulate, who cause genuine and documented harm. They exist. They deserve every consequence that finds them. Nobody with intellectual seriousness is disputing this.

But here is what rarely gets said with equal force: those men are not common. They are not the norm. They are not the story of most households, most marriages, most relationships. They are the exception that has been allowed to define the rule, and that inversion is doing catastrophic damage to how we think, legislate, and teach young people about love, family, and commitment.

The average man in Kenya is a romantic. He will work a job he hates to keep his children in school. He will go without so his family does not have to. He will stay through difficulty, through seasons of nothing, through a wife who has stopped trying, because he believes the family is worth more than his comfort. These men are everywhere. They are invisible in the conversation. And they are the ones this article is about.

What the Narrative Says. What the Ground Shows.

There is a version of the story that society tells, and it has been told so many times it now feels like fact. It goes like this: women are the constant victims of relationships, men are the variable risk, and any system that makes women more independent and more protected is self-evidently an improvement on what came before.

Walk through Eastlands with that story and see how long it holds.

In Zimmerman, there is a man who spent fifteen years building. A plot in Ruiru. A business in Gikomba. Two children he would die for. At fifty-two, after a divorce he did not initiate, he sleeps in one room, pays school fees he cannot confirm reach his children, and starts from zero. His story does not trend. It does not become a sermon illustration. It becomes background noise, like matatu accidents. We mourn briefly and move on.

What the narrative never asks is this: what did he do wrong? In most cases like his, the honest answer is nothing that justified what happened to him. He was not abusive. He was not absent. He simply reached a point where the other party decided the relationship no longer served her, and the legal and social architecture of the country handed her the tools to dismantle everything they built together, including his access to his own children.

This is not an edge case. It is a pattern. And a society that cannot name it clearly, for fear of seeming unsympathetic to women, is not actually protecting anyone. It is only protecting the narrative.

Most men will sacrifice their happiness for their family. Most women, when given economic and legal independence without corresponding cultural expectation, will sacrifice the family for their happiness. This is not a condemnation of women. It is a description of what incentive structures produce.

The Denmark Problem, Brought Home

A study in Scandinavia tracked what happened to family formation when men and women received income increases. Men who earned more tended to start or expand families. Women who earned more tended to slow or stop family formation. The researchers reached for nuanced interpretations. But the data did not need nuance. It needed honesty.

Now consider what we actually observe in Nairobi. A man who makes money in Gikomba buys a plot in Ruiru and starts looking for a wife. A man who builds a business in Thika Road starts thinking about how many children he can now afford. Male prosperity has historically been outward-facing, directed toward something beyond the man himself.

Now consider the wealthier woman. This is not an attack. It is an observation that is statistically consistent and that people discuss privately but rarely in public. How common is it for a financially successful Kenyan woman to marry a man who earns less than her and stay? How common is it for her to look a poor man's way when she has options? There are women who have done exactly this, who married a man with nothing and stayed when he lost everything. They exist. They are extraordinary. And they are about as common as gold in a riverbed. You can find the gold. It is genuinely there. But you do not build a financial plan around finding it.

Meanwhile the man who takes a woman with nothing and makes her comfortable, who builds her up from the ground, who invests in her career and her confidence, only to find himself replaced once that investment pays off for her, is so ordinary a story that it has its own informal vocabulary in every language in this country.

The question is not whether this is fair to women as a category. The question is whether it is honest as an observation, and whether a young man who has watched this pattern play out in his own family, his uncles, his neighbors, his church, is being irrational when he factors it into his decisions.

The Radical Feminist Partner Question: It Is Not Just About the Son

When a young man says he would not want a partner with radical feminist ideology, the response he usually receives is a variation of: you are intimidated by strong women, you want someone you can control, you have been brainwashed.

None of those responses engage what he actually said.

The concern is not that she is successful or educated or opinionated. The concern is about what that ideology does to a family from the inside. It is not only about how a son is raised, though that matters enormously. It is about how a husband is valued. How a family is treated as a unit. Whether the household is a partnership or a competition. Whether sacrifice is mutual or directional.

A woman who has genuinely absorbed the idea that traditional femininity is oppression, that dependence on a man is weakness, that her individual fulfillment is the highest value in any calculation, does not become a different person when she gets married. She brings that framework into the home. And what it tends to produce is a household where the man is expected to provide, protect, and serve, while receiving the message that needing anything in return is patriarchal entitlement.

This is not theoretical. It is what men across this city describe in private conversations they would never have in public because the public conversation has no room for it.

The woman who stays when her husband loses his job. The woman who does not compare him to a wealthier man. The woman who values the household above her own mobility. The woman who pours into her children rather than outsourcing that labor. These are not descriptions of a doormat. They are descriptions of someone who has decided that family is worth more than the alternatives, and who acts accordingly. That woman exists. She is not common enough to bet everything on meeting her by chance.

Common Objection

"But plenty of men leave too. Plenty of men are unfaithful, absent, and uncommitted."

Yes. And the man who does those things forfeits the argument. He is not who this article is about, and conflating him with the man who did everything right and still lost everything is precisely the rhetorical move that keeps this conversation from going anywhere honest. The question is not whether bad men exist. The question is what happens to good men in a system that was not built with their protection in mind.

Preferences Are Not Prejudice

A twenty-two year old man at a Nairobi campus tells his friends he would not want a partner who drinks heavily, parties regularly, or sits significantly above him in socioeconomic status. His peers tell him he is afraid of strong women. They call him controlling. They call him brainwashed.

Let us be precise about what he actually said.

He did not say she is inferior. He did not say she should not do those things. He said he would not choose that combination for his own life and family. That is a preference. And in every other context, preferences of this kind are not only tolerated but celebrated. A woman who says she would not date a man who drinks is considered to have standards. A woman who says she only wants a man who earns above a certain income is considered to know her worth. The moment a man articulates his equivalent preferences, the vocabulary shifts. He is now controlling. He is threatened. He is not ready for a strong woman.

The inconsistency is not subtle. It is so obvious that pointing it out has become socially dangerous, which is itself a signal that something in the conversation is being protected rather than examined.

The preference for a woman who has not had to shed her femininity to succeed is not a preference for weakness. Many women in highly competitive environments have had to develop traits that serve the boardroom but create friction in the home. Aggression, emotional self-containment, the reflexive need to dominate outcomes. These are adaptive in one context and corrosive in another. A man who recognizes this and factors it into his choices is not confused. He is observant.

And the preference for a woman who does not drink heavily and party constantly is not moralism. It is pattern recognition. The lifestyle is a reasonable proxy for certain values, certain priorities, certain answers to the question of what a person thinks their twenties are for. He is not her judge. He is simply a person making a choice about his own life, which is what everyone is entitled to do.

Common Objection

"Patriarchy also harmed men. So your nostalgia for traditional structures is misplaced."

This is true and worth saying clearly. Patriarchy as it actually existed did not benefit the average man. It benefited high-status men. The average and low-status man had different concerns: he died in wars, he worked in mines, he was expendable in ways that were not only accepted but celebrated as masculine virtue. While women were denied the vote and university access, low-status men were denied the most basic thing: the right to survive. This is not an argument for returning to anything. It is an argument against the selective memory that treats historical gender arrangements as having been a conspiracy by all men against all women. The men who made those rules were not the men who suffered their consequences. That distinction matters.

The Economics of Who Invests in Whom

There is a pattern visible enough in Nairobi that it barely needs stating, yet somehow never makes it into the formal conversation.

A man with money tends to make the woman in his life more comfortable. He buys land. He pays school fees. He upgrades her life as a natural extension of improving his own. The prosperity flows outward. This is not universal. There are men who hoard, who spend on themselves alone, who use money as control. They exist. They are not most men.

Now run the experiment in reverse. A woman reaches financial stability. Does the man in her life benefit at a comparable rate? Does she seek out a man who needs building up and build him? Does she marry down in income and stay? Some women do. They are remarkable and they are rare. The statistical pattern points consistently in the other direction: female economic independence tends to raise the minimum acceptable standard for a male partner rather than lower it. Getting richer makes her more selective, not more generous with her selectivity.

Again, this is not an argument that women should be kept poor or dependent. It is an observation about what the data shows when money moves in different directions. And it is directly relevant to the question of which forms of social investment produce the broadest returns for families, children, and communities.

The Court, the Boy, and the Question of Fairness

In 2024, Kenya's High Court lifted a provision that had effectively criminalized consensual sexual activity between teenagers of similar age. The ruling recognized something that should have been obvious: that when two people of comparable age make the same decision, prosecuting only one of them is not child protection. It is selective enforcement that happens to fall consistently on the male party.

When a young man observed publicly that this ruling seemed fair, that it was unjust to send a teenage boy to prison for an act his peer participated in equally, he was told he was unintelligent and threatened with being blocked.

The principle he was articulating has a name in law: symmetry of culpability. If two people consent to an act, and both are below the age of full legal responsibility, the moral logic of punishing only one of them collapses. The practical logic collapses too. Criminalizing the teenage boy does not reduce teenage pregnancy. It does not stop actual predators who target children. What it does is create a legal mechanism that destroys young male lives for situations that involve no victim in the conventional sense.

The response he received was not a counter-argument. It was a declaration that the opinion itself was disqualifying. This is a pattern that runs through every topic in this article. The argument is not engaged. The person making it is dismissed. And somehow the dismissal is treated as the rebuttal.

Advocating for fairness toward boys and men is not unintelligent. It is not a sign of having been brainwashed. It is not evidence of hostility toward women and girls. It is the same moral instinct that drives advocacy for any group being treated unequally under systems that claim to be neutral.

The question is never whether bad men exist. The question is whether the systems we build to address them are calibrated carefully enough to leave good men standing.

The Shamba Test: Risk, Soil, and the Irreversibility of a Bad Season

A farmer in Murang'a does not plant his entire harvest on unfamiliar soil because someone tells him the soil looks good. He tests. He plants a portion. He watches. He protects the remainder of his seed because a bad season with everything staked is not just a setback. It is a reset. Less time. Less energy. Worse options. Starting again on ground you already lost.

This is the logic a young man in Nairobi applies when he thinks carefully about the woman he will build a family with. Not because he has decided all soil is bad. Because he understands that the stakes of this particular decision are not recoverable in the normal sense. A bad business partnership can be dissolved with loss but not ruin. A bad marriage, in the legal and social architecture of contemporary Kenya, can cost a man his children, his property, his community standing, and the years it took to build all of them.

The man who audits before signing a joint venture is not afraid of business. He is professional. The same standard, applied to the decision that will shape the rest of his life, is not fear. It is seriousness. And seriousness about your own future is not something that should require a defense.

Why This Conversation Is Frowned Upon, and What That Tells Us

There is something worth naming directly about the social response to conversations like this one.

When these observations are made in public, the response is rarely a counter-argument. It is a category assignment. Red-pilled. Bitter. Misogynist. Threatened. Not ready for a strong woman. These labels function as a gate. They are designed to make the speaker doubt themselves and to signal to any audience that the ideas do not deserve engagement.

But the effectiveness of a label as a silencing tool has no relationship to whether the underlying observation is accurate. The man who points out that most men who lose in family court did not deserve to lose is not making an anti-woman argument. He is making a pro-fairness argument. The man who says he prefers a partner with traditional values is not declaring war on modernity. He is making a personal choice. The man who notices that female economic independence correlates with lower marriage and birth rates is not advocating for female poverty. He is reading a trend.

A society that cannot discuss these observations without immediately reaching for the shame vocabulary is a society that has decided some people's pain is legitimate and some people's pain is a political inconvenience. That is not a healthy society. It is a society that has replaced honesty with approved narratives, and is surprised when the people excluded from those narratives stop participating in them.

The Honest Conclusion

Most men are romantics. This needs to be said loudly and clearly because it is true and it almost never gets said. Most men want to love well and be loved back. Most men want to build something that outlasts them. Most men would rather sacrifice for their family than walk away from it. The men who do not are real, but they are not the majority, and treating them as the defining category has been one of the most damaging intellectual mistakes of the last generation.

The young man who screens his choices carefully, who has preferences and defends them, who asks questions about incentive structures and legal risk before committing everything he is building, is not broken. He is not behind. He is not a product of internet radicalization. He is paying attention to what he has actually seen happen to the men around him, and he is making a calculation that protects what he has not yet built but fully intends to.

The Murang'a farmer protects his seed not because he hates the soil. He protects it because the seed is the whole point. Bad soil does not apologize for the harvest it ruins. And a man who understands this, who chooses carefully and builds seriously and loves fully within a framework that protects what he loves, is not in need of correction.

He is, in the fullest sense of the phrase, a man who knows what he is doing.
